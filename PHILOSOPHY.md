## The Problem This Solves

Most AI-assisted sessions feel productive. They produce fluent summaries, working code, and a sense of momentum. But the output is often **performance of understanding** rather than understanding itself.

When the chat closes, the ideas dissolve. The user did the work — but the work landed nowhere. There is no structure that persists, no map of how thinking moved, no honest account of what was known versus what was borrowed.

The Strategic Labelling Framework exists to solve this.

---

## The Core Principle: Epistemic Honesty

Every label in this framework carries exactly one epistemic origin marker:

- `∃` — self-generated. You brought this into existence.
- `⊨` — received. Someone or something taught you this.
- `⊕` — co-instantiated. This emerged between you and an AI.

The `⊕` marker is the most important innovation in the framework. It does not pretend AI was not involved. It does not erase the user's role. It marks the ideas that emerged *between* two kinds of intelligence — which is a more accurate account of how thinking with AI actually works.

Honesty about origin is not pedantry. It is the foundation of a knowledge system that can be trusted over time.

## The Garden → EGAP Model

Ideas do not arrive fully formed. They pass through stages:

```
∅  ∘  ∃  ∘  ∃f  ∘  ∃F  ∘  ∃F+  ∘  §
```

**∅ — The Garden**
Ground state. Undifferentiated potential. Ideas exist here before they have been named or noticed.

**∃ — Crosses into existence**
The moment an idea becomes articulable. Not yet structured, but present.

**∃f — Emerging**
Partial. The shape is visible but the edges are undefined. Something is missing.

**∃F — Well-formed**
The idea is coherent. It has a logical structure. It can be related to other ideas.

**∃F+ — Complete**
Stable. The idea has been tested, extended, or implemented. Nothing essential is missing.

**§ — Foundational**
The idea has become load-bearing. Other ideas depend on it. It is no longer just an idea — it is infrastructure.

Each label is a snapshot of where an idea sits on this continuum. `⊕` marks the moments AI accelerates the transition. The user's honesty in applying these markers accurately is what gives the system its integrity.

## The Relational Layer: Why Symbols, Not Arrows

Most knowledge systems use arrows. Arrows are ambiguous. They mean everything and therefore mean nothing.

This framework uses logical and mathematical relations instead:

- `⊢` — yields / proves. A strong directional claim.
- `≅` — isomorphic. Two things have the same structure, even if they look different.
- `⊂` — contained by. This is a subset of that.
- `∈` — belongs to. This is an element of that.
- `∘` — composition. One thing transforms through another.

The discipline of choosing the *right* relation forces clarity. If you reach for `⊢` but realise you mean `⊂`, you have learned something about the idea. The symbol selection is itself a thinking tool.

## The Curry-Howard Connection

The framework was not designed with Curry-Howard in mind. It arrived there naturally.

The Curry-Howard isomorphism states that a proposition and a type are the same thing, and that a proof and a program are the same thing. To write a well-typed program is to construct a logical proof.

The framework's completeness markers map directly onto this:

- `∃F` — a proposition exists. The type is defined.
- `⊣` — typed but unproven. The type signature exists; the program body does not.
- `∃F+` — proven. The program exists and the type is satisfied.
- `∄F` — incomplete. The proposition has gaps. It cannot yet be typed.

This means the framework does not merely organise ideas. It creates **proof obligations**. An idea labelled `⊣` is a formal claim that something needs to be built. An idea labelled `∄F` is a formal claim that something needs to be resolved before building can begin.

The framework makes incompleteness visible *before* a single line of code is written. This is the gap that most development processes leave to bugs to discover.

## More Than Performance

The deepest purpose of this framework is to give the user confidence that their efforts are more than performance.

Performance feels like: *I talked to an AI and got an answer.*

Substance feels like: *I have ∃F+ items I could not label last week that I can label today.*

The completeness stages make epistemic growth legible — to the user, not just to a system. The `∃` marker attributes ideas to their origin. The graph of `⊢` and `≅` relations is a structure that persists beyond any single session.

A session that uses this framework does not just produce output. It produces a **map of how thinking moved**. Over time, that map becomes a second brain — not a storage system, but a reasoning system. One that reflects the user's actual cognitive history, honestly labelled.

---

## What This Framework Is Not

It is not a productivity system. It does not optimise for output velocity.

It is not a filing system. The relational layer is not taxonomy — it is logic.

It is not a replacement for thinking. The labels are prompts to think more precisely, not shortcuts around thinking.

It is a **protocol for epistemic integrity** — a way of ensuring that the work done in a session is real, attributed, and cumulative.
